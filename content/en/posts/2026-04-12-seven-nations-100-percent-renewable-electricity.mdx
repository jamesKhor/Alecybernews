## Executive Summary
Seven nations—Albania, Bhutan, Nepal, Paraguay, Iceland, Ethiopia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo—now generate 100% of their electricity from renewable energy sources, according to data compiled by the International Energy Agency (IEA) and International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA). This milestone, reported by *The Independent*, highlights a significant shift in national energy infrastructure, though the reliance is predominantly on a single, geographically dependent source: hydropower. The achievement underscores the technical and geopolitical realities of a renewable-powered grid, including inherent resilience benefits and potential single points of failure.

## Technical Analysis
The technical foundation for this 100% renewable status is not a diversified mix of wind, solar, and storage, but rather a heavy, and in some cases exclusive, reliance on hydropower. According to the source data, nations like Bhutan, Nepal, Paraguay, and the DRC generate essentially all their electricity from hydroelectric dams. Iceland utilizes a mix of hydro and geothermal. Albania is also heavily hydro-dependent, while Ethiopia's grid is powered by approximately 99% hydro with minor contributions from wind.

From a cybersecurity and infrastructure resilience perspective, this creates a distinct profile. A hydro-dominated grid benefits from a degree of inherent physical security and operational technology (OT) isolation compared to fossil fuel plants with complex supply chains. However, it also centralizes risk. The grid's stability becomes contingent on the security and continuous operation of a limited number of critical generation assets and their associated control systems (ICS/SCADA). A coordinated cyber-physical attack or a major natural disaster affecting water supply could pose a disproportionate threat to national electricity supply. The lack of noted diversification into distributed wind and solar at scale suggests these grids may lack the generation redundancy found in more mixed systems.

## Threat Actor Context
While this report does not detail an active cyber campaign, the energy sector remains a premier target for state-sponsored and criminal threat actors. The concentration of generation capacity in these seven nations could make their hydroelectric infrastructure an attractive target for adversaries seeking to cause widespread disruption. Threat groups known for targeting industrial control systems (ICS), such as those linked to state-sponsored campaigns tracked under names like **Sandworm** or **Berserk Bear**, have historically demonstrated capabilities against energy grids. The motivation could range from geopolitical coercion to demonstrating capability, particularly for nations where these resources are also key economic exports.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
For the nations highlighted and any organization managing similar critical renewable assets, we recommend a defense-in-depth strategy tailored to OT environments:
1.  **Segment and Harden OT Networks:** Ensure strong segmentation between IT corporate networks and OT control networks. Implement robust firewalls and unidirectional gateways where possible to prevent lateral movement from IT compromises.
2.  **Secure Remote Access:** Mandate phishing-resistant multi-factor authentication (MFA) for all remote access to control systems, and limit access through jump hosts with strict logging and monitoring.
3.  **Supply Chain Vigilance:** Vet all third-party vendors and service providers with access to the control environment. Ensure software and hardware components for turbines, governors, and PLCs are obtained through secure channels and monitored for integrity.
4.  **Incident Response Preparedness:** Develop and regularly test incident response plans that assume compromise of critical control systems. Plans must include manual, offline operational procedures to maintain grid stability during a cyber event.
5.  **Diversification Planning:** While not a direct cybersecurity control, long-term energy security strategy should consider diversifying the generation mix (e.g., adding geographically distributed solar/wind) to reduce the single-point-of-failure risk inherent in a hydro-dependent system.
