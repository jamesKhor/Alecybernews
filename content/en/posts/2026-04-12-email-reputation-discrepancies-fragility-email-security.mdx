## Executive Summary
A detailed technical case study demonstrates that major email providers like Gmail can and do block legitimate email traffic even when commercial reputation services report near-perfect scores. The incident, documented by a software company, reveals a critical disconnect between third-party reputation metrics and the proprietary, opaque filtering algorithms used by large mailbox providers. This discrepancy poses a significant operational risk for businesses reliant on email communication and underscores the fragility of modern email delivery ecosystems.

## Technical Analysis
The core issue stems from a fundamental asymmetry in email reputation systems. As detailed in the source blog post, the company utilized multiple commercial email reputation and deliverability services (e.g., Validity, Twilio SendGrid), all of which reported a 99% or higher sender reputation score. Concurrently, their Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting, and Conformance (DMARC) reports showed near-perfect alignment and authentication rates. Despite these positive external indicators, a significant portion of their emails to Gmail addresses were being silently blocked or placed in spam folders.

The investigation found no clear technical fault on the sender's side: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC were correctly configured, IP addresses were not listed on public blocklists, and complaint rates were minimal. The blockage appeared to be driven entirely by Gmail's internal, undisclosed reputation system, which weighs factors beyond traditional authentication and blocklist status. The company's hypothesis, formed through testing, suggests that Gmail's system may heavily penalize senders for low or inconsistent engagement metrics (e.g., users not opening emails) and potentially aggregate reputation across related infrastructure in non-transparent ways. This creates a "black box" problem where senders lack the diagnostic data needed to remediate issues flagged by the provider's proprietary algorithms.

## Indicators of Compromise
None identified at this time.

## Tactics, Techniques & Procedures
While not a malicious attack, the scenario illustrates procedural techniques relevant to email deliverability and security:
* **T1589.001: Gather Victim Identity Information:** Large mailbox providers continuously gather engagement data (opens, clicks, replies, spam reports) to build sender profiles.
* **T1659: Subvert Trust Mechanisms:** The inherent trust in third-party reputation services is subverted when their metrics do not align with the decisive filtering actions of primary providers.
* **Opaque Filtering:** The use of proprietary, machine-learning-driven filtering models that do not provide specific failure reasons or remediation steps to senders.

## Threat Actor Context
This is not an activity attributed to a specific threat actor. The "threat" in this context is systemic, arising from the concentrated market power and operational secrecy of major email platform providers. Their control over inbox placement functions as a de facto gatekeeping capability that can disrupt legitimate business operations without clear recourse, a dynamic that could potentially be exploited by adversaries through sophisticated poisoning attacks on sender reputation.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
To mitigate the risk of opaque email filtering:
1.  **Diversify Communication Channels:** Reduce critical dependence on email for user onboarding or transaction completion where possible.
2.  **Implement Gmail Postmaster Tools:** Verify your domain in Google Postmaster Tools to gain some, albeit limited, visibility into reputation and complaint rates.
3.  **Proactively Manage Engagement:** Segment and clean email lists aggressively. Implement re-engagement campaigns for inactive subscribers before they negatively impact sender reputation. Avoid sending to persistently unengaged addresses.
4.  **Treat Reputation as Holistic:** Assume that reputation is attached to multiple factors, including domain, IP blocks, and "related sender" clusters. A problem with one service or IP could affect others.
5.  **Advocate for Transparency:** Support industry efforts for more standardized, transparent feedback loops from large mailbox providers.
