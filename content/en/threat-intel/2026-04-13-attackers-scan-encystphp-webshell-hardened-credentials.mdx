## Executive Summary
Threat actors are conducting widespread scanning campaigns to locate the "EncystPHP" webshell on compromised web servers. The significant shift in this activity is the attackers' deployment of the shell with hardened, non-default credentials, moving away from easily guessable passwords. This evolution is a direct response to defenders and researchers publishing common credentials, forcing attackers to adapt their tactics to maintain access.

## Technical Analysis
The scanning activity targets the EncystPHP webshell, a file uploader and backdoor written in PHP. According to analysis by Fortinet in January 2025, this tool provides attackers with a web-based interface to execute commands, upload/download files, and browse the server's file system. The current scanning wave, detected in the wild, is notable because it seeks instances where the attacker has manually set a strong password during deployment, rather than relying on the shell's built-in default credentials. This indicates that the attackers behind these scans are likely the same operators who previously installed these shells, returning to check on their persistence or to use them for follow-on operations. The scanning requests are characterized by specific HTTP `POST` parameters sent to potential shell locations.

## Indicators of Compromise
None identified at this time.

## Tactics, Techniques & Procedures
The primary technique observed is **Active Scanning (T1595)**. Attackers are systematically probing potentially compromised web servers for specific resources known to host the EncystPHP shell. This falls under the broader tactic of **Discovery (TA0007)**, as the actors are attempting to rediscover their own implanted tools across a wide victim set. A key procedural change is the shift from using default credentials to employing customized, strong passwords for the webshell's admin interface. This aligns with the technique **Modify Authentication Process (T1556)**, specifically sub-technique **Web Session Cookie (T1556.002)** or password modification, to evade simple credential-guessing defenses and blacklists.

## Threat Actor Context
The specific threat actor or group conducting these scans is not identified in the available data. The activity suggests a pragmatic, financially motivated actor or botnet operator rather than a highly sophisticated state-sponsored group. The act of scanning for their own tools implies a operational security (OPSEC) failure or a deliberate trade-off, accepting the network noise of scanning in exchange for reliably re-establishing connections to a large pool of previously compromised assets. The adaptation to hardened credentials demonstrates they are aware of public defensive reporting and are capable of modifying their toolkit in response.

## Mitigations & Recommendations
Organizations should conduct thorough sweeps of their web servers for unauthorized PHP files, particularly in upload directories or locations commonly exploited in web attacks. File integrity monitoring (FIM) and web application firewalls (WAFs) should be configured to alert on or block requests to known malicious paths and patterns associated with webshell communication. Crucially, defenders must not rely solely on blacklists of known default webshell passwords; any webshell detection logic must account for the possibility of attacker-set credentials. Regular patching of web applications and frameworks is essential to prevent the initial compromise that leads to webshell installation. Server-side logging should be reviewed for anomalous `POST` requests to unfamiliar PHP files.
